How You Can Prevent Social Engineering Attacks—No Tech Skills Required!

Sept. 24, 2024 read Cybersecurity

In today’s world, cyberattacks aren’t just about high-tech hacking tools. Many start with a simple trick known as social engineering, where scammers use deception to manipulate people into handing over their information. But here's the good news: you don’t have to be a cybersecurity expert to protect yourself from social engineering. With a few practical habits, you can stay one step ahead of these scams.

1. Be Cautious of Unexpected Requests

Scammers often pose as familiar figures—your bank, your boss, or even a government agency—to ask for sensitive info. If a request feels out of the blue, trust your gut. Legitimate organizations will never ask for personal details, like passwords, over email or phone.

2. Double-Check Links Before Clicking

Phishing emails often contain links to fake websites meant to steal your information. Before clicking, hover over any link to see where it actually leads. If you’re unsure, don’t click! Instead, type the website’s URL directly into your browser.

3. Verify Before Sharing Information

If someone reaches out for personal information, take a minute to verify who they are. Use contact info from an official source—not what’s in a suspicious message—to confirm if the request is real.

4. Be Mindful of Urgent or Emotional Messages

Social engineers love to create a sense of urgency, hoping you’ll act before you think. Whether they’re saying your account’s at risk or asking for urgent help, take a deep breath. Scammers count on quick, emotional reactions to cloud judgment.

5. Set Up Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

MFA requires a second form of verification, like a code sent to your phone, before you can access your account. Even if someone gets your password, they’d still need that code, adding an extra layer of security.

6. Keep Learning and Stay Informed

Social engineering tactics keep evolving, so stay in the loop about the latest scams. Follow reliable cybersecurity resources and learn how to spot potential threats. Awareness is your best defense.
